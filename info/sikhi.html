What is Sikhi ?

Sikhi, also known as Sikhism, is a monotheistic religion that originated in the Punjab region of South Asia during the late 15th century. It was founded by Guru Nanak Dev Ji, the first of ten Sikh Gurus, and is based on his teachings and those of the nine successive Gurus.

Key Beliefs of Sikhi:

  • Oneness of God: Sikhs believe in one formless, timeless, and all-pervading God, referred to as Waheguru. This belief emphasizes that God is the same for all people, regardless of religion.
  • Equality: Sikhi teaches the equality of all humans, regardless of race, gender, caste, or religion. This principle is foundational, and Sikhs are encouraged to treat everyone with respect and dignity.
  • Selfless Service (Seva): Sikhs are expected to engage in seva, or selfless service, to help others and serve the community without expecting anything in return. This service can be in the form of helping others in need, volunteering, or supporting community initiatives.
  • Honest Living: Sikhs are encouraged to live honestly and earn their livelihood through hard work and integrity. This principle, known as Kirat Karni, emphasizes the importance of living a truthful and ethical life.
  • Naam Japna (Meditation on God's Name): Sikhs are encouraged to meditate on God's name and remember God at all times. This practice helps in staying spiritually connected and focused on the divine.
  • The Guru Granth Sahib:: The Guru Granth Sahib: is the central religious scripture of Sikhi. It is considered the eternal Guru by Sikhs and contains the teachings of the Sikh Gurus as well as writings from various saints and scholars of different faiths.

Practices in Sikhi:

  • Gurdwara: The Sikh place of worship is called a Gurdwara. It is open to people of all faiths and serves as a community center where Sikhs gather for prayer, service, and community meals (Langar).
  • Langar: A communal meal served in the Gurdwara, where everyone sits together and eats the same food, emphasizing equality and community spirit.
  • The Five Ks (Panji Kakar): Sikhs who are initiated into the Khalsa (a community of committed Sikhs) wear five articles of faith:
    • Kesh: (uncut hair)
    • Kangha: (a wooden comb)
    • Kara: (a steel bracelet)
    • Kachera: (cotton undergarments)
    • Kirpan: (a ceremonial sword)

The Sikh Gurus:

The ten Sikh Gurus played a crucial role in shaping the religion and guiding their followers. They provided spiritual leadership and established the principles that Sikhs continue to follow today. The tenth Guru, Guru Gobind Singh Ji, declared the Guru Granth Sahib: as the eternal Guru before his passing.

Sikhi Today:

Sikhi is one of the youngest major religions in the world, with millions of followers globally, particularly in Punjab, India, where it originated. Sikhs are known for their distinct identity, charitable works, and commitment to the principles laid down by their Gurus.

Who Are the Gurus?

The Sikh Gurus are the spiritual leaders who established and developed Sikhism, each contributing to the religion's core teachings and principles. There are ten Sikh Gurus in total, each playing a pivotal role in the evolution of Sikhi (Sikhism).

The Ten Sikh Gurus

  • Guru Nanak Dev Ji (1469–1539)

    • Founder of Sikhism: Guru Nanak Dev Ji was the first Sikh Guru and the founder of Sikhism. He emphasized the oneness of God, equality among all human beings, and the importance of meditation on God’s name.

    • Key Teachings: Rejected the caste system and rituals, promoting a direct relationship with God through devotion and ethical living.

    • Famous Quote: "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim, so whose path shall I follow? I shall follow the path of God."

  • Guru Angad Dev Ji (1504–1552)

    • Second Guru: Guru Angad Dev Ji was the second Sikh Guru, appointed by Guru Nanak Dev Ji.

    • Key Contributions: Developed the Gurmukhi script, which became the writing system for the Punjabi language and the script for Sikh scriptures.

    • Emphasized: Physical fitness and sports to encourage a balanced life.

  • Guru Amar Das Ji (1479–1574)

    • Third Guru: Guru Amar Das Ji was known for his emphasis on social equality and the eradication of caste distinctions.

    • Key Contributions: Established the practice of Langar (community kitchen) as a central part of Sikh worship and emphasized the role of women in religious and social life.

    • Famous Work: Compiled the first official Sikh code of conduct (Rahit).

  • Guru Ram Das Ji (1534–1581)

    • Fourth Guru: Guru Ram Das Ji is remembered for founding the city of Amritsar, which later became the spiritual center of Sikhism.

    • Key Contributions: Laid the foundation for the Harmandir Sahib (Golden Temple) and composed numerous hymns that are included in the Guru Granth Sahib:.

  • Guru Arjan Dev Ji (1563–1606)

    • Fifth Guru: Guru Arjan Dev Ji was the first Sikh martyr, and his leadership marked a period of significant growth for the Sikh community.

    • Key Contributions: Compiled the Adi Granth, the holy scripture of Sikhism, which later became the Guru Granth Sahib:. Also completed the construction of the Harmandir Sahib.

    • Martyrdom: Executed by Mughal authorities, becoming a symbol of resistance against oppression.

  • Guru Hargobind Ji (1595–1644)

    • Sixth Guru: Guru Hargobind Ji introduced the concept of Miri and Piri, the dual responsibility of temporal (worldly) and spiritual authority.

    • Key Contributions: Militarized the Sikh community by introducing the wearing of two swords representing Miri (political power) and Piri (spiritual authority) and constructing the Akal Takht, a seat of temporal authority.

    • Role: Defended the Sikh community from Mughal persecution.

  • Guru Har Rai Ji (1630–1661)

    • Seventh Guru: Guru Har Rai Ji was known for his compassion, particularly in preserving the environment and promoting the welfare of others.

    • Key Contributions: Continued the militarization of the Sikh community but preferred peaceful resolution to conflicts. Also maintained the tradition of Langar.

    • Emphasized: The importance of humility and service.

  • Guru Har Krishan Ji (1656–1664)

    • Eighth Guru: Guru Har Krishan Ji became the Guru at a very young age and was known for his wisdom and compassion.

    • Key Contributions: Served the suffering during a smallpox epidemic in Delhi and earned the title "Bala Pritam" (child saint).

    • Martyrdom: He succumbed to smallpox at the age of eight while serving others, becoming the youngest Sikh Guru to attain martyrdom.

  • Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji (1621–1675)

    • Ninth Guru: Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji was known for his teachings on the importance of freedom of belief and his stand against religious persecution.

    • Key Contributions: Encouraged people to maintain their faith and identity in the face of oppression. His hymns emphasized the futility of worldly attachment.

    • Martyrdom: Was executed by Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb for defending the religious rights of Hindus, symbolizing the Sikh commitment to justice and human rights.

  • Guru Gobind Singh Ji (1666–1708)

    • Tenth Guru: Guru Gobind Singh Ji was the last of the ten Sikh Gurus in human form. He is best known for founding the Khalsa in 1699, a collective body of initiated Sikhs.

    • Key Contributions: Introduced the Five Ks (Kesh, Kangha, Kara, Kachera, Kirpan) as symbols of the Khalsa identity. Declared the Guru Granth Sahib: as the eternal Guru before his passing.

    • Legacy: Transformed the Sikh community into a martial force to protect the oppressed and uphold justice.

The Eternal Guru: Guru Granth Sahib:

After Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the Guru Granth Sahib:, the holy scripture of Sikhi, was declared the eternal Guru. It contains the teachings of the Sikh Gurus and other saints, and Sikhs continue to regard it as their spiritual guide.

These ten Gurus laid the foundation for Sikhism, each contributing to its philosophy, practices, and community structure, ensuring that Sikhi remains a vibrant and guiding force for millions around the world.

Guru Granth Sahib

Historical Context

The Guru Granth Sahib:, also known simply as the Granth Sahib, is the central religious scripture of Sikhism. It is considered the eternal Guru by Sikhs and holds a place of utmost reverence in Sikh worship and life.

Compilation

  • Adi Granth: The Guru Granth Sahib: was initially compiled by the Fifth Sikh Guru, Guru Arjan Dev Ji, in 1604. This compilation, known as the Adi Granth, contained hymns and writings of the first five Sikh Gurus as well as various Hindu and Sufi saints.
  • Expansion by Guru Gobind Singh Ji: The Tenth Sikh Guru, Guru Gobind Singh Ji, expanded the Adi Granth by including the hymns of Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji, the Ninth Guru, and finalized it in 1708. He declared the Guru Granth Sahib: as the eternal Guru of the Sikhs, thus ending the line of human Gurus.

Preservation and Transmission

  • Manuscripts: The Guru Granth Sahib: is preserved in various manuscripts and printed editions. It is meticulously copied and maintained according to strict religious standards.
  • Respect: The scripture is treated with the highest respect, often placed on an elevated platform in Gurdwaras (Sikh temples) and covered with ornate cloths.

Content and Structure

Language

The Guru Granth Sahib: is written in Gurmukhi script and uses several languages, including Punjabi, Hindi, Sanskrit, and Persian. The primary language is Punjabi.

Composition

  • Hymns: The text consists primarily of hymns (Shabads) and poetic compositions, which are meant to be sung. These hymns are written in various ragas (musical measures).
  • Authors: It includes the writings of the Sikh Gurus and several saints from different backgrounds, such as Hindu and Sufi saints, reflecting the universality and inclusiveness of Sikhism.

Organization

  • Granth: The Guru Granth Sahib: is divided into 1,430 pages (Angs). Each page is referred to as an "Ang" (limb), and the text is organized by the ragas in which the hymns are set.
  • Sections: It is divided into various sections, including:
    • Japji Sahib: The morning prayer composed by Guru Nanak Dev Ji.
    • Rehras Sahib: Evening prayers.
    • Kirtan Sohila: Night prayers.
  • Hymns of Other Saints: The scripture includes hymns from various saints like Bhagat Kabir, Bhagat Namdev, and Bhagat Ravidas, contributing to the interfaith spirit of the text.

Philosophical and Spiritual Themes

  • Oneness of God: The Guru Granth Sahib: emphasizes the oneness of God (Waheguru), who is formless, timeless, and beyond human comprehension. It teaches that God is the same for all humanity, transcending religious boundaries.
  • Equality and Social Justice: The text advocates for the equality of all human beings, regardless of caste, gender, or religion. It calls for the elimination of social injustices and emphasizes the importance of living a truthful and just life.
  • Meditation and Devotion: Meditation on God's name (Naam Japna) is a central practice. The hymns encourage devotion, remembrance of God, and the pursuit of spiritual enlightenment through meditation and singing the divine praises.
  • Service and Humility:
    • Seva (Selfless Service): The scripture stresses the importance of selfless service to others. Sikhs are encouraged to engage in acts of kindness and charity without expecting anything in return.
    • Humility: Humility is a key virtue, and the text teaches that pride and ego should be abandoned in favor of a humble and service-oriented life.

Rituals and Practices

  • Recitation and Singing:
    • Kirtan: The hymns are sung or recited in Gurdwaras and during personal worship. The practice of Kirtan (singing of hymns) is a central aspect of Sikh worship.
  • Daily Prayers: Sikhs recite portions of the Guru Granth Sahib: daily, including the Japji Sahib in the morning, Rehras Sahib in the evening, and Kirtan Sohila at night.
  • Respect and Handling:
    • Handling: The Guru Granth Sahib: is handled with the utmost reverence. It is placed on a raised platform known as the Takht or Palki and is covered with a rumala (cloth).
    • Etiquette: Worshippers remove their shoes and cover their heads when approaching the Guru Granth Sahib:. It is treated with great respect, and its physical form is considered sacred.

Significance in Sikhism

  • Eternal Guru: Guru Granth Sahib: is regarded as the eternal Guru of the Sikhs. After the passing of Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the scripture was declared the final and permanent Guru, embodying the spiritual guidance for Sikhs.
  • Guidance and Teaching: It serves as a comprehensive guide to spiritual and ethical living. Its teachings influence all aspects of Sikh life, from daily practices to moral and spiritual values.
  • Interfaith Harmony: By including writings from various saints and emphasizing universal truths, the Guru Granth Sahib: promotes interfaith harmony and tolerance.

Understanding Rāgs in Guru Granth Sahib

Definition of Rāgs:

A Rāg is a framework for musical composition and improvisation. It consists of a specific scale (a set of notes) and a set of rules that guide how these notes should be used to create a melody. Rāgs are designed to evoke particular emotions, such as devotion, peace, longing, or joy.

Rāgs in the Guru Granth Sahib:

The Guru Granth Sahib contains 31 main Rāgs, with some additional minor Rāgs, in which its hymns are composed. These Rāgs are arranged in a specific order and serve as the organizational structure of the scripture. The hymns within each Rāg are further classified by the specific Guru who composed them.

Each Rāg in the Guru Granth Sahib has a specific significance and is associated with a particular time of day, season, or mood.

Purpose and Significance:

The use of Rāgs in the Guru Granth Sahib serves to elevate the spiritual experience of the recitation or singing of the hymns. The Rāgs help convey the emotions and spiritual teachings embedded in the hymns, making the message more impactful and immersive. The Rāgs also reflect the Gurus' deep understanding of the spiritual and psychological effects of music.

The Role of Kirtan:

Kirtan, the singing of hymns from the Guru Granth Sahib to the accompaniment of musical instruments, is a central practice in Sikh worship. The Rāgs guide how these hymns are to be sung, ensuring that the music complements the spiritual message of the words. Traditionally, Kirtan is performed in a Gurdwara (Sikh place of worship), and the specific Rāg for a particular time or occasion is chosen to enhance the congregation's spiritual connection.

The 31 Main Rāgs in Guru Granth Sahib

  • Sri Rāg: Evokes a mood of humility and detachment, often used for devotional and contemplative hymns.
  • Mājh: Associated with the early morning and promotes a mood of peace and contentment.
  • Gauri: A complex Rāg that expresses longing and devotion, used for hymns of praise and reflection.
  • Āsa: A Rāg that conveys hope and encouragement, often sung during the early morning.
  • Gujari: Reflects a mood of longing and separation, expressing deep emotional devotion.
  • Devgandhari: Evokes a sense of devotion and submission to the divine will.
  • Bihāgra: Expresses happiness and contentment, often associated with marital and domestic bliss.
  • Vadahans: Used for hymns that express joy, especially in a communal or festive setting.
  • Sorath: Conveys a mood of confidence and joy, often used for hymns that praise the divine.
  • Dhanasri: Associated with a mood of spiritual joy and fulfillment.
  • Jaitsri: Evokes a mood of calmness and serenity, suitable for meditative hymns.
  • Todi: Expresses a sense of yearning and devotion, often used for deeply emotional hymns.
  • Bairāri: Reflects a mood of sadness and separation, often used in hymns expressing a longing for the divine.
  • Tilang: Conveys a mood of quiet joy and devotion, often sung during the evening.
  • Suhi: Associated with a mood of love and devotion, often used for hymns about the divine marriage.
  • Bilāval: Evokes a mood of happiness and contentment, suitable for hymns of praise.
  • Gond: Reflects a mood of calmness and detachment, often used in hymns about spiritual wisdom.
  • Rāmkali: Conveys a mood of renunciation and spiritual transformation.
  • Nat Nārāin: Associated with a mood of devotion and worship, suitable for hymns praising the divine.
  • Māli Gaura: Evokes a mood of joy and festivity, often used in celebratory hymns.
  • Mārū: Conveys a mood of spiritual strength and courage, often used for hymns of divine praise.
  • Tukhāri: Reflects a mood of longing and spiritual devotion.
  • Kedāra: Evokes a mood of serenity and devotion, suitable for meditative hymns.
  • Bhairav: Associated with a mood of awe and reverence, often used in hymns about divine power.
  • Basant: Conveys the mood of spring and renewal, often sung during the season of Basant (spring).
  • Sarang: Reflects a mood of joy and devotion, suitable for hymns of praise and worship.
  • Mālār: Associated with the rainy season, evoking a mood of spiritual longing and joy.
  • Kānra: Evokes a mood of deep devotion and yearning for the divine.
  • Kalyān: Reflects a mood of joy and spiritual bliss, often used in hymns of praise.
  • Parbhati: Conveys a mood of early morning peace and spiritual awakening.
  • Jaijavanti: Evokes a mood of devotion and love, often used in hymns about the divine.

Bhagats

Bhagats are saints or devotees whose spiritual hymns (Bani) are included in the Guru Granth Sahib, the holy scripture of Sikhism. These Bhagats came from various religious, social, and geographical backgrounds, and their teachings emphasize devotion to God, equality of all people, and the rejection of caste and ritualism.

The Bhagats in Guru Granth Sahib

There are a total of 15 Bhagats whose hymns are included in the Guru Granth Sahib. Below is a list of these Bhagats along with a brief description of each:

1. Bhagat Kabir (1440-1518)

  • Background: Bhagat Kabir was a weaver by profession and a prominent mystic poet. He is one of the most quoted saints in the Guru Granth Sahib.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Kabir emphasized the oneness of God and rejected religious rituals, caste distinctions, and the worship of idols. His hymns express a deep longing for a direct experience of the divine.

2. Bhagat Ravidas (c. 1450-1520)

  • Background: Bhagat Ravidas was born into a low-caste family and worked as a cobbler. He is known for his teachings on equality and the rejection of the caste system.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Ravidas advocated for a casteless society and emphasized that one's devotion to God is more important than social status. His hymns highlight the importance of humility and service to others.

3. Bhagat Namdev (1270-1350)

  • Background: Bhagat Namdev was a Hindu tailor and a prominent figure in the Bhakti movement. His devotion to God and his hymns are highly regarded in Sikhism.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Namdev's hymns emphasize the omnipresence of God and the futility of ritualistic practices. He advocated for a personal connection with the divine through devotion and love.

4. Bhagat Farid (1173-1266)

  • Background: Bhagat Farid, also known as Baba Farid, was a Sufi saint from Punjab. His hymns are some of the oldest in the Guru Granth Sahib.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Farid's Bani focuses on the transient nature of life and the importance of remembering God. His verses are reflective and often discuss the themes of mortality and the spiritual journey.

5. Bhagat Bhikhan (1480-1573)

  • Background: Bhagat Bhikhan was a Muslim Sufi saint known for his devotional poetry.

  • Teachings: His hymns emphasize the unity of God and the importance of love and devotion in achieving spiritual enlightenment.

6. Bhagat Dhanna (1415-1518)

  • Background: Bhagat Dhanna was a simple farmer from Rajasthan. He is remembered for his unwavering devotion to God despite his humble origins.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Dhanna's hymns reflect his deep faith and devotion to God, demonstrating that sincere devotion transcends material wealth and social status.

7. Bhagat Sadhna (1180-1291)

  • Background: Bhagat Sadhna was a Muslim butcher by profession who later renounced his trade to become a devotee of God.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Sadhna's hymns in the Guru Granth Sahib emphasize the idea of surrendering oneself to God and the transformative power of divine love.

8. Bhagat Pipa (1425-1520)

  • Background: Bhagat Pipa was a Rajput king who renounced his throne to become a devotee of God.

  • Teachings: His hymns advocate for the renunciation of worldly attachments and the importance of devoting oneself entirely to God.

9. Bhagat Ramanand (1360-1470)

  • Background: Bhagat Ramanand was a Hindu Vaishnav saint and the guru of several Bhagats, including Bhagat Kabir and Bhagat Ravidas.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Ramanand's hymns emphasize devotion to God without distinctions of caste, creed, or religion, advocating for a universal approach to spirituality.

10. Bhagat Sain (1390-1440)

  • Background: Bhagat Sain was a barber in the court of a king who became a saint through his devotion.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Sain's hymns reflect his belief in the equality of all people and the importance of serving God through honest work and devotion.

11. Bhagat Beni (15th century)

  • Background: Little is known about Bhagat Beni's life, but his hymns are noted for their philosophical depth.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Beni's hymns in the Guru Granth Sahib focus on the futility of ritualistic practices and the need for true devotion and humility.

12. Bhagat Jaidev (12th century)

  • Background: Bhagat Jaidev was a renowned poet and scholar, best known for his work "Gita Govinda."

  • Teachings: His hymns in the Guru Granth Sahib emphasize the themes of divine love and the soul's longing for union with God.

13. Bhagat Surdas (1478-1583)

  • Background: Bhagat Surdas was a blind poet and singer, famous for his devotional songs dedicated to Lord Krishna.

  • Teachings: His hymns included in the Guru Granth Sahib focus on the deep love between the soul and God, often expressed through the metaphor of the love between Radha and Krishna.

14. Bhagat Trilochan (1267-1335)

  • Background: Bhagat Trilochan was a Hindu saint known for his devotion to God and his emphasis on the importance of a pure heart.

  • Teachings: Bhagat Trilochan's hymns in the Guru Granth Sahib stress the importance of living a life of truth, humility, and devotion to God.

15. Bhagat Paramanand (13th century)

  • Background: Bhagat Paramanand was a Hindu saint whose hymns express deep love and devotion to God.

  • Teachings: His Bani in the Guru Granth Sahib focuses on the importance of divine love and the soul's desire to merge with the Supreme Being.
